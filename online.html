The Digital Sanctuary Checklist

This quick checklist will help you prepare for your online therapy sessions, so you can focus on healing, not hardware:/p>

  • Good physical space: Choose a quiet area and comfortable seating, have a light source (like a window or lamp) in front of you, which helps your therapist see your facial expressions and somatic cues, and have room to move around when engaging in embodiment work if needed.
  • Stable Internet Connection: Ensure your Wi-Fi is reliable to avoid disruptions during a video call, close background apps beforehand to save bandwidth, and keep your device plugged into a power source.
  • Supportive objects: Have a glass of water, a box of tissues, and perhaps a "grounding object" (like a smooth stone, a weighted blanket, or a fidget tool) within arm's reach.
  • Minimize Distractions: Use headphones to enhance clarity of conversation and privacy, turn off notifications on your devices, and inform household members of your session time.
  • The "Transition" Buffer: Log in five minutes early to test your camera, microphone, and headset, use this time to breathe and set an intention

Secure & Convenient Online Counseling

Virtual or online psychotherapy, often called teletherapy, offers a modern and flexible way to access high-quality mental health support. For many, it removes the logistical and emotional barriers that often stand in the way of starting therapy.

Benefits of Online Therapy:
  1. Safety and Comfort of Your Own Space: For therapies focused on Trauma and Attachment, the environment matters. Being in your own home allows you to:
    • Utilize "Glimmers": You are surrounded by your own "glimmers"—objects, pets, or scents that signal safety and help you stay grounded during difficult emotional work.
    • Lower Initial Anxiety: The "clinical" feel of an office can sometimes trigger a stress response. Virtual therapy allows you to settle into a familiar chair or hold a favorite blanket, making it easier to open up.
    • Immediate Decompression: After an intense session, you don’t have to navigate traffic or public transit. You can immediately move into a restorative activity, like making tea or taking a walk, which helps in processing the work.
  2. Increased Consistency and Attendance: One of the best predictors of therapeutic success is consistency. Virtual therapy makes it much easier to keep your appointments because:
    • No Commute: You save the time and stress associated with travel, parking, and traffic.
    • Scheduling Flexibility: Sessions can more easily fit into a lunch break, between childcare duties, or around a busy work schedule.
    • Accessibility: For those with mobility issues, chronic illness, or living in remote "therapy deserts," virtual sessions ensure that location or physical ability is never a barrier to expert care.
  3. Enhanced Privacy and Reduced Stigma: Compared to in-person visits, you may feel more secure knowing you won't run into anyone they know in a waiting room or parking lot.
    • Total Anonymity: You have complete control over who knows you are attending therapy.
    • Discreet Care: If you live in a small community or have a public-facing career, teletherapy provides a level of separation and privacy that is deeply reassuring.
  4. Broadened Access to Specialists: By choosing virtual therapy, you are no longer limited to the therapists within a 10-mile radius of your home.
    • Expert Matching: You can work with a specialist in Creative Embodiment or Relational Psychotherapy who truly fits your needs, even if they are located across the state.
    • Continuity of Care: If you travel for work or move to a different city within the same jurisdiction, you can often keep your therapist, maintaining the vital relationship you’ve built.
Is Virtual Therapy as Effective?

Yes. Extensive research shows that for the vast majority of concerns—including PTSD, anxiety, and depression—virtual therapy is just as effective as in-person treatment. Our therapists are specifically trained to adapt somatic and experiential techniques for the screen, ensuring that the "relational bridge" remains strong even through a digital connection.
